What is a “payment chain” in card payments?

A payment chain is the full path your card transaction takes from the cardholder to the merchant’s bank account and back – including all the intermediaries that touch the data and the money flow.

Even a “simple” card payment usually involves five core parties:

  • Cardholder – the person using the card.
  • Merchant – the business accepting the payment.
  • Acquirer – the merchant’s bank or PSP.
  • Card network – Visa, Mastercard, Amex, local schemes, etc.
  • Issuer – the bank or fintech that issued the card.

The payment chain describes how these parties connect, how messages travel between them and where fees, chargebacks, risk checks and delays enter the picture.

How a card payment moves through the chain

While implementations vary between providers, most card payments follow a similar high-level flow:

  • 1. Capture – the card details are captured via terminal, POS, wallet or online checkout.
  • 2. Authorization request – the acquirer sends an auth request along the network to the issuer.
  • 3. Risk & controls – issuer checks balance, limits, fraud signals, 3DS/SCA rules and more.
  • 4. Auth response – approved or declined, plus codes that explain why.
  • 5. Clearing & batching – approved transactions are grouped and sent for clearing.
  • 6. Interchange & scheme fees – card network and issuer fees are calculated.
  • 7. Settlement – funds are moved between banks and arrive in the merchant’s payout account.
  • 8. Post-transaction events – refunds, chargebacks or disputes may travel back along the chain.

Merchants and fintechs rarely see all of this directly, but small changes in any link of the chain can have a visible effect on approval rates, costs and customer experience.

Different chain models in card payments

Not all payment chains look the same. Depending on the scheme and setup, the structure can change:

Four-party schemes

Classic card model with separate issuer and acquirer connected via an open network (e.g. Visa, Mastercard). Common for most debit and credit cards.

Three-party schemes

Card, issuer and acquirer are controlled by the same brand (e.g. some Amex or store cards), simplifying the chain but limiting reach.

Aggregator / PSP models

Payment service providers bundle acquiring, routing and risk, adding an extra node in the chain while simplifying integration for merchants.

On-us vs off-us

When issuer and acquirer are the same bank (“on-us”), processing can be faster and cheaper. Off-us requires external clearing and more hops.

Card-present vs CNP

In-store chip & PIN / tap vs. online or in-app payments. Card-not-present (CNP) adds extra risk checks and often 3DS/SCA into the chain.

Wallet & tokenized chains

Apple Pay, Google Pay and other wallets insert tokenization and wallet providers into the chain, improving security and changing how data flows.

Where fees, risks and limits appear in the chain

Each link in the chain can introduce cost, risk controls and limits. Understanding where they sit helps explain why a transaction is expensive, slow or declined.

Chain link Typical fees / costs Risks & limits
Merchant & acquirer Merchant service fee, gateway/PSP fees, terminal or platform fees. Merchant category (MCC) restrictions, volume limits, rolling reserves.
Card network Scheme fees, cross-border fees, assessment fees. Rules on surcharging, acceptance, dispute handling and compliance.
Issuer Interchange fees, FX margins when currency conversion is involved. Credit limits, daily spend limits, fraud rules, SCA/3DS policy.
Settlement & payouts Payout fees, currency conversion, faster-settlement premiums. Payout delays, reserve balances, risk-based settlement schedules.
Post-transaction Chargeback fees, retrieval fees, operational dispute handling costs. Fraud exposure, friendly fraud, regulatory and scheme compliance.
